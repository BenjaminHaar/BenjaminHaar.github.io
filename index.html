The Demographics of the Opioid Crisis

Benjamin Haar

May 15, 2021


Introduction


Opioids are both a miracle of modern medicine and a scourge. Without painkillers, many modern medical and dental procedures and their aftermath would be intolerable. Opioids have proven to be strong and effective painkillers. On the other hand, over 50,000 lives were lost due to opioid abuse during 2019 alone (https://www.cdc.gov/drugoverdose/data/statedeaths.html). Drug addiction has been a major health issue in the United State for many years. Opioids, in particular synthetic opioids, currently dominate drug overdose deaths. The problem of opioid addiction will persist long after Covid-19. Unlike Covid-19, there is no vaccine to make it go away.

This project is motivated by compassionate concerns and practical implications. As mentioned in the opening paragraph, many people are hurt by opioids. Any insight gained about demographic factors contributing to opioid addiction could alleviate some of this pain experienced by many Americans. A practical implication of this study is to determine whether we are fighting the right battle against opioid addiction. Currently, a lot of attention is being given to stopping the production of opioids. However, if socioeconomic forces are driving the epidemic, then stopping the production of opioids will not solve the problem. Either illegal synthetic opioids or other drugs will take its place. Another practical implication is that prescriptions for opioids have been dramatically reduced in recent years. For example, it is typical for a dentist to prescribe 1 day of opioids after a painful dental procedure. If physical addiction to a short-term course of opioids is not a leading cause of opioid abuse then perhaps the amount of opioids prescribed by doctors or dentists could be revisited.

The purpose of this project is twofold: provide you with some insight on the demographics of the opioid crisis and, at the same time, give a brief tutorial on the various pieces of data science. This is intended to be a "working" tutorial; that is, most of the tutorial will be in the form of an interesting case study. I also want to present my personal perspectives and observations on data science. In particular, I will discuss why I made certain choices in the course of this project.

Before discussing the details of this project, I want to offer some background material on the tools used in this work. This project is written in the Python language, a great language for data analysis. For those people unfamiliar with Python, I recommend the official site at https://docs.python.org/3/. The latest reference documentation is available at https://docs.python.org/3/reference/index.html.

All data handling in this project is done using Pandas. Pandas is built on top of Python and allows sophisticated manipulation of tabular data. It provides the basic data structures and functionality needed for various stages of data analysis. More information can be found at the official website https://pandas.pydata.org/. The user guide at https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/user_guide/index.html is written as a tutorial. Another reference that I highly recommend is the book, Python for Data Analysis (McKinney, Wes. O'Reilly Media. 2018), written by the originator of Pandas. This book provided me some insights on why certain things in Pandas work the way they do (e.g., indexing). From my personal experience, one of the difficulties in learning the techniques of data science is that redundant functionality exists between Python, Pandas, and various libraries (e.g., NumPy). McKinney's book does an excellent job of sorting out the roles of Pandas relative to Python and the other libraries.

I. Data Collection, Curation, and Representation

Part A: Census Data


A major part of data science is the collection, curation, and representation of data. The first steps are to find relevant data and then to load the data into your program. In this project, Pandas will provide the basic data structure for organizing and handling the data. In general, the data must be cleaned and processed. Cleaning can involve filling in missing data or removing them. Processing could entail filtering outliers. Next, data wrangling is performed (http://www.jeannicholashould.com/tidy-data-in-python.html). This involves combining data (join, merge, etc) and, sometimes, pivoting data.

When I began this project, I was hoping to use the 2020 Census data. Unfortunately, the 2020 Census data pertaining to this project are not available at this time. So, instead, I used the 2019 Census data, the most recent data available. However, there is a silver lining to using 2019 data rather than 2020 data. The 2020 data, when released, will be heavily influenced by the Covid-19 pandemic. The Covid pandemic has masked the effects of the opioid crisis. For comparison, Covid killed over 1/2 million Americans over the last year while opioid abuse killed about 50,000 Americans in 2019. As the Covid pandemic recedes, we will still be stuck with a painful opioid crisis similiar to 2019.

In this project, I chose to use primary sources, the Census Bureau and the CDC. Earlier, I looked at some aggregated datasets on the internet (e.g., kaggle.com) but they seemed to be of dubious quality. A great benefit of using primary sources that are widely used is that they have already undergone extensive tidying/cleaning. On the downside, one can get lost in the extraordinary volume of available data at these sites. In particular, the Census API is extremely granular and some classifications are overlapping.

The Census supplements its 10 year survey with the American Community Survey (ACS). The contents of the 2019 ACS is described at https://www.census.gov/data/developers/data-sets/acs-1year.htmlDescription. Census data for this project was extracted using the detailed API at https://api.census.gov/data/2019/acs/acs1/variables.html. The codes that are listed in the following block are from this page. It was difficult to identify the proper codes because of the unusually large number of codes associated with any given demograpic characteristic. However, downloading was straightforward once the proper codes were identified. One minor glitch is that the Census Bureau now requires you to have a key to download data.

Missing Data


Eleven states do not break out their opioid overdoses from total drug overdoses (indicated by NaN's in the above table). Of these states, three are populous (Florida, Michigan, and Pennsylvania). Collectively, this is a lot of missing data. It is not surprising because there is significant variability in reporting of opioid deaths among states (ref).

Fortunately, all states do report total drug overdoses ('all drugs' column in the above table). I propose that we use the complete set of total overdoses to fill-in the missing opioid data. To do this, we need to find a relationship between opioid overdoses and all-drug overdoses. The following analysis makes use of plotting routines from the matplotlib library and a polynomial fitting routine from the NumPy library.

Figure 2. This plot shows a linear relationship between opioid overdoses and all-drug overdoses. Three least squares lines are computed with all data (red), data without outliers (green), and data without outliers and constrained to have zero intercept (blue). See discussion for why there are so many least squares lines.

As mentioned in Figure 6, three least squares lines were computed. The first LS line was computed with all of the data. After looking at the data, two points stand out as outliers (Texas and California). The presence of outliers is not surprising because of the inconsistency in data collection and reporting among states (ref). The second LS line excludes these outliers. With this improved LS line, another problem became apparent in the analytics section. Some data were predicted to have negative opioid cases. This was puzzling but was resolved by constructing a third LS line which is constrained to have zero intercept. One last problem was that some LS algorithms (e.g., polyfit() of NumPy) can't constrain the line. So I used a more general linear algebra method (i.e., lstsq() of NumPy) to calculate the third LS line. The constrained LS line has a high R-squared. The LS coefficients of this line (m, b) are used to interpolate values for states with missing opioid data.

III. Hypothesis Testing and Machine Learning

Hypothesis: Demographic data can predict the opioid death rate by state.


I want to determine whether specific demographic characteristics of the general population can predict the prevalence of opioid overdoses. The go-to method for fitting polynomial functions is polyfit() from the NumPy library. But polyfit() can't be used on multivariate problems, as we have here. So, instead, I will implement linear regression with machine learning.

With an R-squared of 0.475, this is not a model to sound the trumpets. P-values for each coefficient are given in the column P>|t|. The p-values for the coefficients of the individual attributes (male, median age, and advanced degree) appear to be significant at the 95% confidence level. However the larger model is rejected because of the poor overall fit. Also, the output suggests strong multicollinearity. This was expected because many of the factors are highly correlated. Certainly, more work could be done to refine this model, and this is discussed in the next section on Insights.

IV. Insights


This project has given me several insights into the opioid crisis and data science, in general. Opioid abuse is an ongoing, devastating crisis. This project looked at statewide demographic data. It showed that some attributes have a moderate correlation with the incidence of opioid deaths by state. For example, the median age and advanced degrees in a state has some predictive value. The ideal study would not look at statewide data but rather individual persons with full descriptions of their personal traits. Then, a full-scale machine learning algorithm could be applied.

I looked very hard for relationships in the data but the main result of the model is disappointing. However, at the end of the day, you can't let your preconceived notions dictate your investigation. Instead, you have to listen to the data and let it guide you. The relationship between population demographics and opioid overdoses warrants further investigation. The next steps, in perhaps another tutorial, would be to gather additional information from the Census Bureau and the CDC, and also investigate other data sources. Further investigation could also apply additional tools such as principal component analysis and various machine learning techniques.
